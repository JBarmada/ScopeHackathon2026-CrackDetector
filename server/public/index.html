⚠️ Crack Danger Reference Guide

Shear Crack

Diagonal (≈45°) near supports in beams/columns. Indicates shear force exceeds capacity. High structural risk — can cause sudden brittle failure.

Critical if widening

Flexural / Moment Crack

Vertical cracks at mid-span of beams or at base of columns. Indicates bending stress. Usually predictable; ductile failure mode.

Moderate — monitor width

Shrinkage Crack

Random map-cracking during early curing. Usually shallow (<20mm). Low structural risk unless pattern becomes systemic.

Low structural risk

Corrosion-Induced Crack

Longitudinal cracks along rebar line, rust staining. Steel expansion splits the cover. Progressive — will worsen without intervention.

High — repair urgently

Settlement Crack

Diagonal or stepped cracks from differential foundation movement. Indicates ongoing settlement — active cracks are dangerous.

Investigate foundation

Thermal Crack

Horizontal or vertical, regular spacing. Caused by thermal expansion/contraction cycles. Common in walls without expansion joints.

Usually low risk

Torsional Crack

Spiral or helical pattern around a member. Indicates torsional force exceeding capacity. Rare but very serious.

Critical

ASR (Alkali-Silica)

Map/gel cracking with a white gel exudate. Internal chemical reaction — no cure, only management. Reduces long-term capacity.

Requires specialist
🚨 Cracks in reservoir walls, dam faces, and spillways require IMMEDIATE specialist inspection. Do not rely solely on AI detection for life-safety decisions.

Piping / Internal Erosion Warning Signs

  • Crack accompanied by seepage or discoloured water
  • Sand boils or wet spots on downstream face
  • Sink holes or depressions on embankment crest
  • Increasing seepage flow over time
  • Any cracking + drawdown of water level >300mm unexplained
